## The Multi-Cloud Hype

Every vendor pitch deck mentions multi-cloud. Every CTO wants to "avoid lock-in." But after implementing multi-cloud architectures for several clients, I have learned that **most organizations should not pursue multi-cloud as a primary strategy**.

## When Multi-Cloud Makes Sense

There are specific scenarios where multi-cloud delivers real value:

### 1. Regulatory and Data Sovereignty

When operating in regions with strict data residency requirements, you may need different providers for different regions. For example, a SaaS company serving the EU and the Middle East might use Azure in regions where Microsoft has stronger data residency commitments.

### 2. Workload-Specific Strengths

Some workloads simply run better on specific clouds:

- **Machine Learning at scale**: GCP often wins with Vertex AI and TPU access
- **Microsoft ecosystem**: Azure for organizations heavy on Microsoft 365 and Active Directory
- **Mature serverless**: AWS Lambda still has the deepest ecosystem

### 3. Disaster Recovery Across Providers

For mission-critical systems, having DR in a different provider protects against provider-wide outages (which do happen).

## When Multi-Cloud Is a Mistake

### 1. "Avoiding Lock-In" as the Only Reason

Lock-in concern is real but overstated. The cost of building cloud-agnostic abstractions is usually higher than the lock-in itself. You end up locked into your own abstraction layer.

### 2. Small Engineering Teams

Multi-cloud doubles or triples your operational complexity:

- Two sets of IAM models
- Two cost management approaches
- Two networking topologies
- Two sets of deployment pipelines

If your team is under 20 engineers, single-cloud excellence beats multi-cloud mediocrity.

### 3. "Best of Breed" Without Real Need

Picking S3 for storage, BigQuery for analytics, and Azure AD for identity sounds clever. In practice, the cross-cloud data transfer costs and operational overhead destroy the supposed benefits.

## A Pragmatic Framework

When clients ask about multi-cloud, I use this framework:

1. **Primary cloud**: 90% of workloads
2. **Secondary cloud**: Specific workloads that benefit (ML, compliance)
3. **DR cloud**: Critical systems only

This gives you optionality without operational chaos.

## Closing Thoughts

Multi-cloud is a tool, not a strategy. Use it when the specific benefits outweigh the operational cost. Otherwise, invest in deep expertise on one cloud and ship products instead of managing complexity.
